Optimise

To help your content rank within search engine results pages (SERP's), HubSpot's ‘Optimise’ feature will analyse your content for effective keyword dispersal.

To use this feature, simply place your text cursor into the entry bar and submit your chosen terms (from targeted keyword research), or you can choose from a list of previously used keywords by searching through the suggestions list. However, please try to avoid repeating keywords (if possible), as this can sometimes cause your own content to compete against each other.

Try to use a minimum of two keywords for your content. Once these have been entered a ‘To-Do’ list of four sections will appear (as bulleted below), and each section will be broken down to display where additional keyword information is needed.

  • Content Body
  • Title
  • Meta
  • Image

Note, these sections may vary in order depending on the completion rate of your content.

By default, incomplete to-do’s will be highlighted in orange to start with, but as your content is progressively entered the to-do’s will change from orange to green. So keep an eye on them while entering your text and amend any issues that crop up.

Ch. 4.1 - Creating a Form

To create a Form, navigate to the dashboard and click Contacts > Forms. Please note, you can also read HS’s own guidance on this subject here.

To create a new Form, click the blue button located towards the top-right hand side of the page called ‘Create new form’.

A ‘Create new form’ pop-up will now appear asking you to name your form (for internal use), but be as descriptive as possible, and when finished press ‘Create form’. You’ll now be redirected to the ‘Details’ tab of the main form creator. From here, enter actionable text for your ‘Submit Button Text’ (underneath Form Name), then select the ‘Fields’ tab.

Be aware, you can check how your form looks at any time by pressing the ‘Preview’ button located towards top-middle of the page (below).

From the ‘Fields’ tab, select the fields you need either by using the search-bar, or by expanding the category’s on the far-left by pressing the arrow button, then dragging and dropping the relevant fields into the ‘Default questions’ section (below). You can also drag a ‘Add separator’ across to give each field a custom header.

To edit the fields in your ‘Default questions’ section (below), click the ‘Edit field options’ (pencil icon) button towards the right-hand side of your questions, and a pop-up named ‘Editing Field for Property’ will open.

From this pop-up, you will have options for the following:

      1. Field Configuration

Make field required: The form cannot be submitted without the user having completed this field.

Make field hidden: Hides the field from the user, for internal category setting usage. For example, you could set a hidden ‘persona’ field to support categorisation later on.

Label: The label used for the form field.

Placeholder: Text that appears in the field before anything is typed.

Help Text: Text presented below the field to help the user.

Default Value: Enters a default value into the value, that unless changed by the user will be submitted with the form.

When finished press ‘Done Editing’.

You can also tag your fields as ‘Smart’ by pressing the star icon (below) to the far-right of your fields.

Selecting this option will hide this field if the user has previously answered this question or if the property already has a value on their contact record. The email field will always remain visible on the form, but will be pre-populated with the data from previous submissions.

Next, select the ‘Options’ tab, and complete the following:

  1. Pre-populate fields with known values: Completes the field if the user has known values on their contact record. It’s better practice to use the Smart Fields feature for this.
  2. Disable cookie tracking: When disabled, every submission from the same browser creates a new contact. Be aware, if selected, you cannot use the Smart Fields feature, and page information such as page view history and source data will not be available.
  3. Display Captcha on this form? Display a Captcha on this form to prevent automated form submissions.

       4. Notified when submitted: Entered email addresses will be sent a notification when the form is completed.

 Complete these steps until you have a form you are happy with, then when ready press the blue ‘Save Form’ button towards the top-right of the page.

Ch. 5.1 - Preparing a Blog Post 

 

It’s easier to have a blog post prepared in advance, and then copy it into HS, rather than creating it from scratch within the page editor. However, in order for a blog post to rank in Google, it must contain the following elements to generate organic traffic.

  1. Keywords: 1 primary, and 1 secondary search term (do not reuse terms).

     
  2. Title: <60 characters with spaces – must include the primary search term.

     
  3. Subheadings: Include the primary search term naturally.

     
  4. Main body text: <1000 words – use both search terms multiple times.

     
  5. First Paragraph: Include the primary search term once naturally – twice if possible.

     
  6. Meta description: <21 words - include the primary search term.

     
  7. Image: You will need to prepare and upload a relevant image for your blog post, which includes the primary search term within the file name (Alt Tag). For instructions, please refer to chapter 2. 

     
  8. Call to Action (CTA): You will need to prepare a CTA for your blog post. For instructions, please refer to chapter 3.

Ch. 6.1 - Inbox 

 

The Inbox page provides notifications on what new social interactions your accounts have had (like Twitter and Facebook); displayed through both a numeric counter next to the Inbox link, and text updates from the main-feed.

Ch. 6.2 - Monitoring 

 

The Monitoring page displays what Twitter communications there has been for specific search phrases called ‘Streams’.

Streams are user created and effectively cast a net across Twitter, pulling-in any tweets that contain a particular set of words. Highly useful for monitoring trends and competitors!

You can view an already created Stream either by selecting a named tab like those shown below, or by clicking the ‘More’ option towards the far right-hand side, which shows every stream available.

By selecting a stream, it will display what discussions there have been for its terms, allowing you to monitor areas of interest, brand chatter, and competitor activity.

To create a new stream, click the ‘+’ icon next to the previously discussed ‘More’ link, and follow the on-screen instructions. To edit a current stream, select that particular stream and click ‘edit’ (below), then adjust or add new search phases to the ‘that contain’ text box, then save.

 

Ch. 6.3 - Publishing 

 

The publishing page is useful because it shows how effective previous social updates have been, and allows users to craft better messages as a result.

The main view of the page will show how many clicks and interactions each past update has had. More specifically, clicks indicate (ideally) how many people have clicked the link or image within the message, whilst interactions will include Twitter responses such as retweets, likes, and replies.

The filters for this publishing section are very straight forward.

The ‘Published’ and ‘Scheduled’ tabs will simply show what they describe, whilst the ‘Unsuccessful’ tab will display those updates that failed to auto-publish, with an explanation as to why. In more cases this is very rare, but if this happens, simply reschedule the failed message for another time.

In addition, the ‘Filter Channels’ tab lets you segment social updates by different accounts like Facebook and LinkedIn, the ‘All campaigns’ tab segments by HS campaign, and the ‘All time’ tab is by time period.

Ch. 10.1 - Workflow Preparation 

 

HS describes Workflows as the following: “A workflow is a series of automated actions that you can trigger to occur based on a person’s behaviours or contact information. Workflows go way beyond simple marketing automation, though. With workflows, you can send emails, update contact information, add or remove contacts from lists, and trigger email notifications.”

Therefore to create a Workflow, it’s best to prepare the following elements in advance:

Have a clear objective(s) for your workflow.

Identify the HS segmentation properties or the list membership to enrol your audience.

Identify the HS segmentation properties that will track the completion of your main workflow objective(s) – these may differ from your enrolling requirements.

Draw a flow-chart with a pencil and paper, or try www.lucidchart.com.

Create your workflow content (usually emails). For instructions, please refer to the chapter 7.


 

As an example, here’s how we could employ the five actions above:

We have the objective of getting new CMM contacts to configure an AWS server

We identify that our audience is segmented by the ‘Free Trial Contact List’

We also identify that we can track the main workflow objective through the HS contact property ‘Configured Server’

For clarity, we then create a flow-chart with the following actions and timings (below)

  1.  

Detail the list or segmentation properties of our audience

Send our segmented CMM audience the ‘Configure Server’ email

Add a 3 day delay

We then use the ‘Configured Server’ contact property to track if our users actually configured a server

If Yes - we send them a new ‘Configure Servers to be On-Demand’ email

If No - we resend then another ‘Configure Server’ email

We would then proceed to create this new Workflow within HS

 

However, when creating a new workflow for real; the area that will prove most difficult to users with a low-level of HS knowledge, is making sure to always use the correct HS contact properties. To which there is no shortcut other than familiarising yourself with them.

The best way to achieve this, is to create a test-list for your workflow within the Contacts section, and experiment with contact properties until you’re 100% sure that you’re pulling through the correct user information. For instructions on how to create a list, please see chapter 11.2.

A Standard workflow is triggered when a contact enrols after performing an action such as joining a HS list, visiting a page, or submitting a form.

Ch. 11.1 - Static and Smart Lists 

 

What’s the difference between a static and a smart list?

Static lists: Static lists are created automatically when you import a list into HubSpot or they can be created by selecting a list criteria.  Once a static list is generated, the list members will remain the same unless you add or remove contacts manually (explanation via HubSpot).

Common uses:

Imported data from another database

Email blasts

Internal staff lists

Event/trade show lists

Manually adding contacts to workflows


 

Smart lists: Smart lists offer a flexible way to organize contacts in HubSpot and automate your marketing actions. You can build these automatically refreshing lists based on contacts who meet certain criteria, and removing contacts that no longer meet that criteria (explanation via HubSpot).

Common uses:

Contact property segmentation

Form completions

Workflow completions

Ch. 16 - A/B Testing 

 

Creating an A/B test - HubSpot:

 1. Changing a HubSpot 'Website Page' into a 'Landing Page' :

 

  • To perform an A/B Test, your HubSpot page must be a 'Landing Page'.

To change a website page into a landing page, find your page in 'Website Pages' > click the dropdown menu for ‘more’ (located towards the far right) > choose ‘Move to landing pages’.

A popup will then appear and state a condition of the move, if okay, press the ‘Move to Landing Pages’ button to finish.


 

  2. Setting up a HubSpot Landing Page A/B Test:

  • Click on your Landing Page (via 'Landing Pages) > click 'Edit' > click 'Settings' > click 'Create and A/B Test' - give your variation a (easy and accurate) name > click 'Okay'
    • This process also works if you've created a new Landing Page.
  • After this change, you'll now see a green/blue 'A/B' icon next to the top-left text for 'Back to landing pages'.
    • Clicking either of these 'A' or 'B' buttons allows you to switch your view between either page.
    • Essentially, treat each page as a seperate entitity, and make your specifc changes. To learn more about creating a landing page, please see the HubSpot Wiki.
    • Note, changes made to one page should not affect the other - unless it's a change to a global template module which your two pages share.
  • To publish a variation, click the blue 'Publish' button on the top-right of each page.
  • To stop or choose a variation winner, click on your Landing Page (via 'Landing Pages) > click 'Variations' > click 'Choose as winner'.

 

 3. Enabling Google Analytics A/B Tracking:

  • To enable Google Analytics tracking, each variation will need a specific piece of code inserted into it's header. 
  • To do this for your 'A' page > open you 'A' page and click 'Settings' > click 'Edit HTML settings' > insert '<meta name="bbmarketing-abtest" content="A">' at the top of the entry box, and before any other code (if present).
  • To do this for your 'B' page > open you 'A' page and click 'Settings' > click 'Edit HTML settings' > insert '<meta name="bbmarketing-abtest" content="B">' at the top of the entry box, and before any other code (if present).
  • To remove this code, simply delete it from the header file.
  • After 1-2 days (after GA has recorded some traffic), these results will show in GA with the end of their URL showing '/?abtest=A' and '/?abtest=A' respectively.
    • Note, I would advise to only have this code implemented across one A/B test at a time. However, if you wish to run additional tests, I would contact Alex Putnam and ask him to adapt the code.
